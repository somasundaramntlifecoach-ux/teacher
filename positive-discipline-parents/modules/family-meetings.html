Family Meetings

Solving problems together, on a regular schedule

Kind
Firm

A short, regular family meeting gives every family member — including children — a real voice in solving recurring problems, planning fun activities, and appreciating each other. It builds the sense that the whole family solves problems together, rather than the parent solving everything alone.

A Simple Weekly Format

1) Appreciations — each person thanks another for something specific. 2) Old business — revisit last week's agreed solutions. 3) New business — bring up problems from a shared agenda list. 4) Plan something fun together. Keep it under 20-30 minutes.

A Shared Agenda Sheet

Keep a notepad or whiteboard where anyone can write a problem during the week, to be discussed calmly at the next meeting — instead of arguing about it in the heat of the moment. This alone reduces many daily conflicts.

Everyone's Voice Counts

Even young children can suggest solutions. Let the family vote on or agree to solutions together — children who help create a solution are far more likely to follow it than one simply imposed on them.

Real-Life Example

Two siblings, aged 9 and 12, fight almost daily over who gets the TV remote or more screen time. Their mother ends up as the judge every single time, and both children grow increasingly resentful — not of each other, but of her decisions.

⚠ The Common Mistake

By becoming the sole judge of every dispute, the mother becomes the target of resentment rather than the conflict being resolved between the siblings themselves — and since no lasting system exists, the same fight repeats daily.

✓ How to Handle It Instead

Bring the recurring remote/screen-time conflict to the weekly family meeting as a standing agenda item. Let both children propose solutions (e.g., a rotating schedule) and vote on one together — a solution they built themselves is far more likely to actually stick.

★ Suggestion to Improve

Keep a small 'family agenda' notepad on the fridge. Any recurring conflict gets written down and deferred to the next meeting, instead of being fought over — or exploded over — in the heat of the moment.

Try This at Home

Hold a 15-minute family meeting this week. Start with appreciations only, and one small item from the agenda.

Key Takeaways

  • Family meetings give everyone, including children, a real voice in solving problems.
  • A shared agenda sheet defuses in-the-moment arguments by deferring them to a calm discussion.
  • Children are more likely to follow solutions they helped create.
