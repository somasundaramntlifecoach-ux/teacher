Managing Your Own Emotions

Time-In, not Time-Out — regulating yourself first

Kind
Firm

You cannot teach calm from a place of chaos. Positive Discipline asks parents to regulate their own emotions first — modeling the very skill we want children to learn — before addressing the child's behavior.

The Positive Time-Out

Traditional time-out isolates and shames a child. A positive time-out is a cozy, self-chosen space (a 'calm corner') that both parent and child can use to regulate feelings before problem-solving — not as a punishment, but as a break to feel better so they can do better.

Your Own Calm-Down Plan

Before you react, try: take three slow breaths, silently count to ten, or simply say 'I need a minute before we talk about this.' It's okay to step away briefly — this models self-regulation rather than suppression or explosion.

Repair After a Misstep

Every parent loses their temper sometimes. What matters most is the repair: 'I raised my voice earlier, and I'm sorry. I was frustrated, but you deserved better. Let's talk about it now.' This teaches children that mistakes can be repaired with honesty and accountability.

Real-Life Example

After an exhausting day at work, a mother snaps and shouts loudly at her 5-year-old over a small spilled glass of milk. She feels guilty within minutes but isn't sure what to do next.

⚠ The Common Mistake

Believing that the moment has passed and it's best to just move on without addressing it leaves the child confused and slightly afraid, without understanding that the outburst was about the parent's exhaustion, not about how much they are loved.

✓ How to Handle It Instead

Once calm, kneel down and repair: 'I raised my voice earlier and I'm sorry — I was tired, but you didn't deserve that. Spilling milk is just an accident.' This models that mistakes can be owned and repaired, which is exactly what we want the child to learn too.

★ Suggestion to Improve

Set up your own designated calm-down spot at home — not just one for the children — and use it visibly when you feel your patience running low, so your child sees healthy self-regulation being modeled, not just talked about.

Try This at Home

Set up a small 'calm corner' at home this week with a cushion, a book, or a soft toy — for anyone in the family to use when feelings run high.

Key Takeaways

  • Regulate yourself first — you cannot teach calm from chaos.
  • A positive time-out is a calming space, not a punishment or isolation.
  • Repairing after losing your temper teaches accountability better than never making mistakes.
