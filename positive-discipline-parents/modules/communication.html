Effective Communication

Listening, I-Messages, and speaking with respect

Kind
Firm

How we speak to our children becomes the voice they eventually use to speak to themselves and, later, their own children. Effective communication in Positive Discipline centers on listening fully and speaking in ways that invite cooperation rather than defensiveness.

Listen First, Fully

Put down the phone, make eye contact, and reflect back what you heard before responding ('So you're saying the game felt unfair'). Children who feel truly heard are far more willing to listen in return.

'I' Messages instead of 'You' Blame

'You always leave your shoes everywhere!' invites defensiveness. 'I feel frustrated when I trip over shoes, because I want our hallway to be safe' names the feeling, the trigger, and the need — without attacking the child's character.

Fewer Words, More Action

Children tune out lectures. Try a single word ('Shoes.'), a gesture, or a written note on the door instead of a long explanation repeated five times. Positive Discipline calls this 'shortcuts' — brief, respectful, and effective.

Real-Life Example

Every evening, a father says, 'Why can't you EVER listen? I've told you a hundred times to keep your room clean!' Over months, his son has stopped responding altogether and simply walks away mid-sentence.

⚠ The Common Mistake

Blame-loaded 'you' statements repeated daily as a lecture cause the child to mentally tune out over time — the brain learns to filter out the familiar pattern of scolding rather than engage with it.

✓ How to Handle It Instead

Replace it with a short 'I' message once: 'I feel stressed when the room stays messy, because I want our home to feel peaceful.' Follow up daily with just one word as a reminder — 'Room' — instead of the full lecture each time.

★ Suggestion to Improve

For one week, try limiting every instruction to under 10 words. Notice whether your child engages more when there's less to tune out of.

Try This at Home

Replace one 'you' statement this week with an 'I feel... when... because I need...' message, and notice your child's reaction.

Key Takeaways

  • Reflective listening — hearing fully before responding — builds cooperation.
  • 'I' messages name the feeling, trigger, and need without blaming.
  • Short 'shortcuts' (a word, a gesture) are often more effective than long lectures.
