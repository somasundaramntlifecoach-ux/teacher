Encouragement, Not Praise

Raising capable, confident children from the inside out

Kind
Firm

Praise ('Good job! You're so smart!') focuses on the person and outcome, and can create approval-dependence. Encouragement ('You worked hard on that and didn't give up') focuses on effort and process, building a child's internal sense of capability that doesn't depend on constant external validation.

The Difference in Practice

Praise: 'You're the best artist!' Encouragement: 'I noticed you used a lot of colors — tell me about your choices.' Praise judges; encouragement describes and invites reflection, helping the child build their own internal evaluation rather than depending on the parent's verdict.

Encourage Effort, Not Just Outcome

Carol Dweck's research on growth mindset aligns closely here: children praised for being 'smart' often avoid challenges to protect that label, while children encouraged for effort and strategy take on harder tasks and recover better from setbacks.

Everyday Encouragement Phrases

'You kept trying even when it was hard.' 'What do you think of how it turned out?' 'I trust you to figure this out.' 'Thank you for your help — it made a real difference.' These build a lasting sense of contribution and capability.

Real-Life Example

After Kavya scores well in an exam, her mother says, 'You're SO brilliant, you'll definitely top the class next time too!' A month later, facing a harder exam, Kavya avoids studying and seems unusually anxious.

⚠ The Common Mistake

Praise tied to the identity label 'brilliant' makes any future struggle or lower score feel like losing that identity, not just a normal learning setback — so Kavya starts avoiding challenge to protect the label rather than to actually grow.

✓ How to Handle It Instead

Instead say, 'You revised every topic steadily this week — that consistent effort is what helped.' This praises something repeatable (effort, strategy) rather than a fixed trait, so a future setback doesn't threaten her sense of self.

★ Suggestion to Improve

Each day this week, name one specific effort you noticed your child make, instead of a general compliment like 'good job' — specificity is what makes encouragement land.

Try This at Home

For one full day, replace every 'Good job!' with a specific, effort-focused encouragement sentence. Notice how it changes the conversation.

Key Takeaways

  • Praise judges the person and outcome; encouragement describes effort and process.
  • Encouragement builds internal capability rather than approval-dependence.
  • Effort-focused language helps children take on challenges and recover from setbacks.
