Understanding Your Child's Behavior

The Four Mistaken Goals — Attention, Power, Revenge, Inadequacy

Kind
Firm

Psychologist Rudolf Dreikurs observed that children who misbehave are usually pursuing one of four 'mistaken goals' — mistaken because the strategy the child uses does not actually get them what they truly need. Recognizing the goal changes how you respond, and how effective your response will be.

Attention — 'Notice me, involve me'

The child seeks constant attention, even negative attention, because they mistakenly believe they only belong when they are being noticed. Your clue: you feel annoyed. Instead of reacting, give attention for positive behavior and involve the child in useful tasks.

Power — 'You can't make me'

The child believes they only belong when they are in control or winning. Your clue: you feel challenged or provoked, and want to fight back. Instead, withdraw from the power struggle, offer limited choices, and invite the child's help in problem-solving.

Revenge — 'I'll hurt back'

The child feels hurt and believes belonging is impossible, so they try to hurt others as they feel hurt. Your clue: you feel hurt yourself. Instead of retaliating, stay calm, build connection, and avoid punishment which confirms the child's belief that they are unlovable.

Assumed Inadequacy — 'I give up'

The child believes they cannot succeed, so they stop trying, protecting themselves from further failure. Your clue: you feel hopeless or want to give up on the child too. Instead, take small steps, show faith in the child's ability, and celebrate tiny efforts, not just results.

Real-Life Example

During homework time, 8-year-old Priya keeps 'losing' her pencil, asking her mother to fetch water, and interrupting every few minutes — always needing something.

⚠ The Common Mistake

The mother calls her 'careless' and scolds her each time she interrupts. Scolding actually rewards the goal of attention-seeking with more attention (even if negative), so the interruptions increase over the following weeks rather than stopping.

✓ How to Handle It Instead

Recognize this as an attention goal. Give Priya 10 minutes of planned, undivided attention right after school before homework starts, so her need is already met. During homework, calmly redirect without extra conversation: 'You have everything you need — I'll check your work after this page.'

★ Suggestion to Improve

For one week, journal which of the four mistaken goals (attention, power, revenge, inadequacy) shows up most in your own child's difficult moments — the pattern usually becomes obvious by day three or four.

Try This at Home

Next time your child misbehaves, pause and notice your own feeling first (annoyed, challenged, hurt, or hopeless) — it usually reveals the goal. Try one matching response from above.

Key Takeaways

  • Misbehavior is a coded message about belonging, not simple defiance.
  • Your own feeling in the moment is a clue to the child's mistaken goal.
  • Attention-seeking: redirect to positive involvement.
  • Power struggles: withdraw from the fight, offer choices.
  • Revenge: stay calm and rebuild connection.
  • Inadequacy: encourage small steps, show faith.
